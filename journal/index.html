February 26, 2024

Game Design Research

For this learning journal, I read the article “Game Design UX Best Practices” by Amir Dori. In the article Dori discusses the best design practices for game apps.

I do not play as many games now as I did when I was younger, so it was a refresher to see all the examples Dori includes throughout the article. Many of his tips are quite interesting and useful, such as ‘Avoid the X’ and when to flash the ‘Rate Us’ pop-up.

The main thing I disliked about the article was how innately capitalistic it is. I understand that at the end of the day, these game companies are businesses who need to profit, which explains why these best practices emphasize monetization. But, almost all of them are elements of dark UX, used to extort as much money as they can from users. It leaves a bad taste in my mouth.

Of course, it is hard to escape that designers are hired to create better user experiences to profit companies. This article makes it clearer for me to avoid these types of industries as much as I can, honestly.

Visual Thinking Analysis

This is an image of a shrine on Miyajima island. This image actually includes a much deeper story as to how I got there. I was studying abroad in Fall ‘23 in the UK, but decided to take a solo trip to Japan nearing the end of the quarter in December. I decided to root myself in three major cities, Tokyo, Kyoto, and Osaka, and take day trips to neighboring cities. I took a 2-hr bullet train from Osaka to Hirashima, then a ferry from Hirashima to the island. It was a perfect day, and although you can not tell from the image, there were many other tourists and even deer there. I was able to stay for the sunset, and it was beautiful.

This image relates to my archive because it was a special moment in my trip, as I had never been on a trip on my own before. The fact that I had gotten to this island reflected my growing independence and confidence.

This image is a collection of my peer’s clothes, accessories, and makeup. The most interesting aspect of this image is how she laid out all of her items in an organized chaotic way. The fact that all of her objects were also color coordinated speaks loudly about her aesthetic. The most obvious aspect of the image is how it tells the story of how my classmate likes to present herself. I can tell she likes to dress femininely. The most mysterious part is how she came about acquiring these clothes and accessories. Since everything is so cohesive, there must have been a time when it was not so orderly, maybe a time when she was still discovering herself.

February 07, 2024

Visual Thinking Strategies

In this day and age, images have never been such a common way to communicate messages. Even with videos, which are thousands of moving images. Though, with the amount of content I consume, I do recognize that I often skip out on learning the context of this media before moving on to the next. This is because the vast amount of information has diminished my ability to focus on what is in front of me. The need for constant stimulation means I want to view the newest visuals out there.

To learn how to remove myself from this habit, the article “10 Intriguing Photographs to Teach Close Reading and Visual Thinking Skills,” by the New York Times was helpful in providing tips. The writing piece was geared towards teachers to implement better image observing methods, but I can also heavily implement them in my life, such as to notice details and expand my knowledge of the world. Prioritizing tiny details in photos gives me time to stop and cherish the time I take to learn. Expanding my knowledge provides greater explanation for an image in a broader context. For example, like in the article, understanding that it does not snow heavily in Ireland, may make images of high snow piles in the country surprising.

A website I found to use images in a visually appealing way is a portfolio site by an Indonesian designer, Reksa Andhika. Moving the cursor around the screen spawns images of his work. Hovering over case study images reveals the inverted colors of the same photos. It is an interesting way to get users to interact with different elements on the page, as you never know what lies underneath.

January 31, 2024

Overlays Design Patterns

I read the article “Best Practices for Modals / Overlays / Dialog Windows” by Naema Baskanderi. I do not have much experience designing overlays, and to be honest, I do not think of them much. I am glad I am gaining more awareness of their existence, because I will inevitably have to design them.

Most times, I do not have any issues with overlays on websites I interact with. The only time they are noticeably annoying is when there is no way to exit out of them without inputting your email address or paying for a subscription. If that happens, I immediately leave the page.

After reading the article, at least now I know what to include and avoid. For example, for accessibility reasons, there should be multiple ways to close the overlay, such as a ‘close’ button, an ‘x’ icon, and if ‘esc’ is on the keyboard clicked. The location of overlays is also important. They should be positioned closer to the top of the screen in case they are cut off on mobile and should not take up more than 50% of the screen. These are all helpful tips to consider.

January 25, 2024

Research Form Design

It's funny that, regardless of how much everyone hates filling out forms, they are still required in so many places. I wonder if we will eventually come up with a better design for them altogether rather than only finding ways to improve the current format.

The article "Best practices for form design" by Salim Ansari was an informative read, though. As a UX designer, it's always my goal to make even the most boring design processes pleasant. Many of the listed practices were familiar to me, such as distinguishing between optional and required inputs, ideal label placement, and providing form feedback. However, practices like asking easy questions first, sizing fields accordingly, and minimizing dropdown menus were refreshingly new tips.

My main takeaway is that to create the best type of form, one in which a user is willing to fill out, you must make it easy and simple. Making a form tab-ready or providing a matching keyboard is a perfect example of this. It's really no different than any other exemplary designs, at least in Western culture. I especially love LinkedIn’s forms as it exemplifies all of the best practices Ansari recommends, from using guiding placeholder text to a productive action button.

From this point forward, I will always keep in mind to NEVER overthink a form. Simple (but instructive) is truly best.
